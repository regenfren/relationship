Being a Relational Manager

Leadership Through Connection

What is Relationship-Oriented Leadership?

Relationship-oriented leadership focuses on building strong interpersonal connections, fostering open communication, and prioritizing the well-being and development of team members. It emphasizes understanding different perspectives, creating trust, and maintaining positive working relationships.

Unlike task-oriented leadership that focuses primarily on goals and efficiency, relationship-oriented managers understand that people are the foundation of success. They recognize that different team members have different perceptions, needs, and communication styles.

The Power of Feedback

One of the core tools of relationship-oriented leadership is effective feedback. Why is it so important?

Different Perceptions

Team members naturally have different viewpoints and interpretations of the same situation.

Bridging Gaps

Perception gaps can widen over time and lead to misunderstandings or conflicts if not addressed.

Building Common Vision

Through open dialogue, teams can align their understanding and work toward shared goals.

How Feedback Works in Relationship-Oriented Leadership:

  • Talk about yourself (I-statements) - Share your perspective without blaming others
  • Express your emotions - Acknowledge feelings and create emotional connection
  • Use an informal approach - Create a comfortable, conversational atmosphere
  • Accept different opinions - Value diverse viewpoints and build consensus

Real-World Examples

Here are interesting ways managers use relationship-oriented leadership in their teams:

1
Weekly One-on-Ones with Personal Check-ins
A manager at a tech startup schedules 30-minute weekly one-on-ones that start with "How are you doing, really?" before diving into work topics. This creates space for team members to share personal challenges, work-life balance concerns, or career aspirations.
Impact: Team members feel valued as whole people, not just workers, leading to increased loyalty and reduced turnover.
2
"Perception Alignment" Meetings
After major projects, a marketing director holds "perception alignment" sessions where team members share what they thought went well, what didn't, and how they interpreted key decisions. The manager facilitates discussion to bridge understanding gaps.
Impact: Prevents conflicts from perception gaps and helps the team learn from each other's viewpoints.
3
Informal "Coffee Walk" Feedback Sessions
Instead of formal performance reviews, a creative agency manager takes team members on walking meetings outside the office. The informal setting encourages honest, emotion-based conversations about work challenges and growth opportunities.
Impact: More authentic feedback exchange and stronger manager-employee relationships built on trust.
4
Team "Emotion Check-ins" Before Meetings
A project manager starts every team meeting with a quick "emotion check-in" where each person shares one word describing how they're feeling. This practice normalizes emotional expression and helps the team understand each other's current state.
Impact: Team members are more empathetic and adjust their communication style based on others' emotional states.
5
"I-Statement" Conflict Resolution
When conflicts arise, a retail manager uses structured "I-statement" sessions: "I felt... when... because..." This approach helps team members express their perspective without defensiveness, leading to collaborative problem-solving.
Impact: Conflicts are resolved faster and relationships are strengthened rather than damaged.
6
Reverse Feedback Sessions
A department head regularly asks team members for feedback on their own management style. They create a safe space where employees can share what's working and what could improve, modeling vulnerability and continuous learning.
Impact: Creates a culture of mutual respect and continuous improvement, with managers learning from their teams.
